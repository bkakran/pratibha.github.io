In 2014, I joined a small taskforce within HCL Technologies, incubated in the CEO's office, with a bold mandate: build an IoT business unit from scratch. What started as a cross-functional team exploring the Internet of Things became IoT WoRKS — a global business unit that would go on to drive $90+ million in bookings, secure strategic partnerships with major cloud providers, and establish HCL as a credible player in the enterprise IoT space. The journey taught me more about product leadership, organizational dynamics, and the art of intrapreneurship than any MBA could.

## Starting with the 'Why' Before the 'What'

The IoT landscape in 2014 was noisy. Every technology company was talking about connected devices, but few could articulate a clear business case. Our first task wasn't building a product — it was building a thesis. We spent weeks doing market research, defining target verticals, and creating pricing models for ten service lines. The GTM strategy we developed during my management internship became the foundation for everything that followed. Lesson: don't start with the technology. Start with the business problem you're solving and for whom.

## The MVP That Changed Everything

Our breakthrough came when we developed an MVP on field services for a leading industrial manufacturer. This wasn't a polished product — it was a working demonstration that connected sensors, cloud infrastructure, and analytics to solve a real operational problem. The result? A $90+ million booking. The lesson here was profound: in enterprise B2B, an imperfect solution to a real problem beats a perfect solution to a theoretical one. We didn't win because our technology was superior. We won because we understood the customer's workflow better than anyone else.

## Building a Partner Ecosystem

No IoT platform succeeds in isolation. Our strategy was to build deep partnerships with cloud providers — Azure, Google Cloud Platform — and co-create solutions. This approach resulted in $400K in direct partner investment and joint go-to-market campaigns. The Partner Development Kit we created accelerated partner onboarding and ensured consistent quality across implementations. Key insight: in platform businesses, your partner ecosystem is as important as your own capabilities. Make it easy for others to succeed on your platform, and success compounds.

## Scaling from Innovation Lab to Business Unit

The hardest transition wasn't building the first product — it was scaling from an innovation initiative to a recognized business unit. This required navigating organizational politics, securing headcount, establishing P&L ownership, and building credibility with sales teams who were accustomed to selling traditional IT services. We succeeded by focusing on revenue. Every quarter, we delivered results that made the business case for continued investment undeniable. By FY19-20, I had exceeded the revenue target of $24M and was the top performer in IoT business development for strategic hi-tech accounts.

## Intrapreneurship Lessons

For product leaders thinking about internal ventures, here's what I'd share. First, treat executive sponsorship as a product — maintain it actively. Our CEO-office incubation gave us air cover, but we had to continuously demonstrate progress to maintain it. Second, build the team before you build the product. The founding members of IoT WoRKS were chosen for their ability to operate in ambiguity, not their domain expertise. Third, celebrate visible wins early and often. The Business Catalyst of the Year award I received at the 2014 Sales Meet wasn't just personal recognition — it signaled to the entire organization that IoT was real, it was working, and it was worth investing in.
