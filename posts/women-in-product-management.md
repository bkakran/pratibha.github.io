I've spent over twelve years building technology products — from IoT platforms in Bengaluru to advertising technology in the Bay Area. Along the way, I've been the only woman in the room more times than I can count. In C-suite pitch meetings, technical architecture reviews, partnership negotiations, and conference panels, I've often looked around and noticed the imbalance without anyone else seeming to. This isn't a complaint piece. It's an honest reflection on what I've learned about leading effectively in environments where you don't always match the default template.

## The Credibility Tax

There's an invisible tax that women in technical product roles pay: the need to over-prove competence before being granted credibility. When a male PM says "I think we should prioritize this feature," the conversation moves to the merits of the idea. When I've said the same thing, particularly early in a new role, the response has sometimes been a more scrutinizing "what's your evidence?" — not because the ask was unreasonable, but because the credibility hasn't yet been assumed.

Over time, I've learned to front-load evidence not because I should have to, but because it's strategically effective. I walk into rooms with data, customer quotes, and competitive analysis ready to go. This preparation has become a superpower: by the time peers are debating opinions, I'm presenting evidence. The credibility tax transforms into a credibility advantage — but only if you're aware of the dynamic and choose to weaponize preparation rather than resent the inequity.

## Finding Your Voice in a Loud Room

Product leadership often rewards the loudest voice, the fastest response, the most assertive stance. These are culturally coded behaviors that many women aren't socialized to adopt — and frankly, shouldn't have to adopt to be effective. I've found my most powerful leadership moments happen not when I match the volume in the room, but when I change the tone.

Asking the clarifying question that nobody else thought to ask. Naming the elephant in the room that everyone is dancing around. Synthesizing three competing viewpoints into a coherent path forward. These are quiet acts of leadership that create disproportionate impact. They require deep listening — a skill that's undervalued in tech culture but indispensable in product management, where understanding unspoken user needs and organizational undercurrents is the entire job.

## Sponsorship Over Mentorship

Early in my career, I sought mentors — people who would give me advice. That was valuable, but what accelerated my career was finding sponsors — people who would advocate for me in rooms I wasn't in. The distinction matters enormously.

A mentor says, "You should apply for that role." A sponsor says, "I know exactly who should lead this initiative," and names you to the decision-maker. Sponsors put their own reputation on the line for your advancement, which is why sponsorship relationships are built on demonstrated results, not just rapport.

My advice to women building their careers in product: invest in doing exceptional, visible work. Make your impact undeniable. Then cultivate relationships with senior leaders who've seen that impact firsthand. Sponsorship follows proof, not potential.

## Building the Table You Want to Sit At

One of the most empowering shifts in my thinking came when I stopped trying to earn a seat at existing tables and started building new ones. When I led the creation of an IoT business unit, I wasn't asking for permission to join someone else's strategy meeting — I was convening my own. When I've driven product strategy for advertising platforms, the cross-functional working groups I established became the decision-making forums that everyone wanted to be part of.

Building the table means defining the agenda, setting the norms, and creating an environment where diverse perspectives aren't just tolerated but structurally required. I now consciously design meetings where the quietest person is invited to speak first, where data is presented before opinions, and where "I don't know yet" is an acceptable answer.

## What I Tell My Younger Self

If I could go back and talk to the version of me who was just starting in tech, I'd say three things. First, your different perspective isn't a liability — it's your competitive advantage. The product insights I'm most proud of came from seeing what the room full of similar backgrounds missed. Second, build your technical depth. Not because you need to prove anything, but because deep technical understanding earns you the respect of engineering teams in a way that no title can. Third, be patient with the industry but impatient with yourself. The culture is changing, but your career won't wait for it. Take the initiative, take the risk, and take up space.
