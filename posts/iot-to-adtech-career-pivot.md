When I moved from leading IoT products to advertising technology at a major retail media platform, people asked if it was a radical change. Sensors to search ads? Edge computing to campaign optimization? On the surface, these domains couldn't be more different. But the truth is, the fundamentals of great product management are universal — and the transition taught me more about leadership than a decade in a single domain ever could.

## The IoT Foundation

My journey in IoT started even before my professional career — as an undergraduate at BMS College of Engineering, my research on 6LoWPAN-based wireless sensor networks in collaboration with the Indian Institute of Science contributed to India's national IoT policy formation and BIS standards. That early exposure to systems thinking — understanding how hardware, software, connectivity, and data analytics must work together — shaped how I approach every product challenge. At HCL, I was a founding member of the IoT Business Unit (IoT WoRKS), incubated within the CEO's office. We built products for field services, asset management, worker safety, and industrial manufacturing. The MVP we developed for a leading industrial manufacturer resulted in $90+ million in booking.

## What IoT Teaches You About Platform Thinking

IoT products are inherently complex. You're dealing with hardware constraints, connectivity protocols, edge computing limitations, cloud infrastructure, data pipelines, analytics engines, and user interfaces — all of which must work in concert. This complexity forces you to think in systems, not features. It teaches you to design platforms, not point solutions. When I transitioned to AdTech, this platform mindset was invaluable. An advertising platform isn't just a campaign builder — it's an ecosystem of bidding engines, analytics dashboards, recommendation systems, and self-serve tools that must work seamlessly together.

## The Pivot: From Devices to Dollars

The move to a leading retail media platform in 2021 was deliberate. I wanted to apply platform thinking to a domain with massive scale and direct, measurable business impact. In IoT, impact was often measured in operational efficiency or cost savings — important, but sometimes abstract. In advertising, the metrics are immediate and unambiguous: ad spend, click-through rates, return on ad spend, revenue. This directness changed how I prioritize. In IoT, we could afford longer iteration cycles. In AdTech, the market moves weekly, and every day a feature isn't live is revenue not captured.

## Transferable Skills

Several skills transferred directly. Stakeholder management across engineering, sales, and executive teams was identical. Data-driven decision-making was foundational in both domains. The ability to translate complex technical concepts into business value for non-technical stakeholders — a skill I honed presenting IoT solutions at Mobile World Congress and IBM Watson conferences — proved essential when aligning cross-functional teams on platform strategy at scale.

## Advice for Product Leaders Considering a Pivot

If you're considering a domain change, here's what I've learned. First, your 'how' matters more than your 'what.' The frameworks, methods, and mindsets you've developed are portable — the domain knowledge you'll learn. Second, look for structural similarities, not surface similarities. IoT and AdTech look different but share deep structural parallels: platform architectures, data-driven optimization loops, and the need to serve diverse user segments with varying sophistication. Third, embrace the beginner's mindset. The fresh perspective you bring as a newcomer is a feature, not a bug. Some of our best product innovations in retail media came from questioning assumptions that 'AdTech veterans' took for granted.
